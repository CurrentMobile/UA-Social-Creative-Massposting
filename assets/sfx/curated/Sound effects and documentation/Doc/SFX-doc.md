When to use sound effect
A deep whoosh. 
0.62s sub-bass whoosh. Energy almost entirely below 330 Hz (centroid 165 Hz). Slow swell up (~0.18s attack), then a quick drop. Reads as a cinematic 'air-push' rumble — felt more than heard. 
Outro transition, Camera push-in on a high-impact line 

Deep woosh. 


0.51s sub-bass whoosh. Even deeper than 'A deep whoosh' — energy concentrated 20–215 Hz (centroid 88 Hz). Highly tonal (autocorr 0.75) — feels like a low engine rev / movie trailer 'braam'. 
Heaviest scene transition, used when the outro (CTA) of the video enters
Camera shutter. 
0.26s mechanical SLR shutter click. Bright (centroid 4.5 kHz), noisy (ZCR 0.51), instant transient 
Camera flash transition, Scene cuts, a fast cut, 
Fast shutter
0.21s shutter click, slightly faster/sharper than 'Camera shutter' (centroid 4.3 kHz, noisy short). 
Used multple times in stack where there are multiple b roll being displayed. For example, when listing something fast and pop ups of the b rolls of those things being listed are displayed on the screen ro a faster paced 'photo cut' transition. This fast shutter should be used on each of the pop ups. Same role as Camera shutter, but for back-to-back rapid snaps.
Click. 
0.13s SHARP bright click (centroid 6.2 kHz, ZCR 0.81). Hard, snappy, percussive transient 
snap cuts, Also good for snap-zoom punches and beat-matched cuts, App logo intro,

Click-sound. 


0.12s muffled mid-treble click (centroid ~3 kHz). Softer than 'Click' — feels like a UI tap, not a hard snap. 
Use when talking about something like "subscribe" or the like.
Fart reverb. 
1.05s raspberry/fart sound with a 0.27s reverb tail. Mid frequency (~1.1 kHz), broadband. 
An absurd answer, or a bad take,

Bell notification. 


1.16s clean bell ding. Strong tonal content at ~2 kHz (autocorr 0.87), 0.53s ring-out tail. Sounds like a hotel front-desk bell or a chat-app ping. 
Used to indicate a win in a game app. This sound effect is used when talking about winning something.
Notification. 
0.37s short low-mid ping. Centroid 669 Hz — warmer/lower than the bell or iPhone ones. Strongly tonal (autocorr 0.95). 
When showing an app interface, Used for when there is to be a more professional feel to the notification. E.g., a Slack/Discord-style ping for a more 'pro' notification feel than the iPhone one 
Notification 1. 
0.89s bright two-tone chirp (centroid 3.9 kHz, very tonal autocorr 0.91, ZCR 0.47 — there's noise riding on the tones). Acoustic fingerprint identical to the old 'Mountain notification' — likely the same file renamed. 
Used for when a game win is being displayed, General-purpose 'soft ping' under a caption pop or a small sticker landing. Use when a Bell or iPhone notification would be too loud/branded. 
Shocked sound effect. 
0.94s sustained orchestral/vocal shock stinger. Mid frequency (centroid ~940 Hz), long attack (0.42s) then a 0.5s decay — builds and falls 
Hard cut to a shocking answer/scene pivot. Also used on the 'wait, what?!' beat. 
Rubber duck
0.38s bright squeaky rubberduck squeeze (centroid 5.7 kHz, very high ZCR 0.76, noisy). Higher-pitched and more chaotic than 'Duck quack' 
Used when someone says something that doesn't make sense. Cartoon comedy beat — drop on a silly visual gag, a wronganswer moment, or a fake 'duck-typed' joke. Pairs with a meme cut-in 


Duck quack
0.16s short mid-frequency quack (centroid 1.7 kHz). Percussive attack, single quick squeak. 
Use to cover when someone use a curse word. Also used when someone says something that doesnt make sense.
Turn table scratch.    
0.7s classic record-scratch — pitch-sliding mid-range (centroid 1.7 kHz), 0.45s sustained decay, low autocorr (the pitch is moving). 
  Use Hard cut to a shocking answer/scene pivot. Also used when you want to put a stop to something. For example, to a particular vibe or energy in the video

 Whoosh swoosh sound. 


0.35s short low-mid whoosh (centroid 414 Hz). Lighter than Deep woosh, heavier than Swipe whoosh 
Swipe transitions, Mid-tier transitions, when a caption blocks flies in, a clip slides into frame, or a soft cut between two B-roll shots. 

Awkward cricket. 


3.8s loop of chirping crickets in the 2–7 kHz band. Tonal/rhythmic, not noisy. Builds and holds (slow 'attack' is just the chirps stacking up). 
Awkward silence beat, drop after a flop joke, a non-answer in an interview, or a beat the speaker meant to land that didn't. Best on a freeze-frame or hold 
Sad trombone
1.96s classic wah-wah-waaah 'sad trombone' fail cue. Mid-low (centroid 1.18 kHz), EXTREMELY tonal (autocorr 0.98) — three clean descending notes. 
Used when there is a sad reaction to a reply or when someone said something sad. Failure / disappointment beat. After a wrong answer, a flop joke, or a 'and that's how I lost everything' moment. THE 'fail' SFX 


Win Game rewards
1.14s bright 'level up' / gamewin jingle. Mid-band (580 Hz – 6.8 kHz), sustained tonal (autocorr 0.77), 0.59s decay tail. 
Used when the person is talking about rewards. Reward / win / earnings reveal beat. Drop on '+$50 earned', a gift-card pop, a 'congrats!' caption, or a points-counter hitting its target. Pairs with Cash register or Ting. 

Cash register. 


0.76s cha-ching of an old mechanical register — bright midhigh band (centroid 3.7 kHz), notable rolling attack (0.48s) as the drawer opens. 
When talking about money. Money-on-screen, payout reveal, gift-card pop-up, anywhere a $ icon hits the frame 

Good idea. 


1.13s ascending 'lightbulb' chime — tonal (autocorr 0.74), mid-band (1.2–3.1 kHz). Sounds like a positive cartoon idea cue. 
Drop on the moment the speaker presents the offer / solution. E.g 'And here's how…' line. 

Swipe whoosh swoosh.      



Very short 0.09s low-mid whoosh (centroid 820 Hz). Almost a sub-blip — quick swipe, not a heavy transition. 
Used when there is a caption strip wipe, sticker slide, or fast transition
Ul data loading sound effect.

Used to indicate when something is counting or loading



Ui loading sound effect 2. 		
4.9s noisier loading wash, narrower band (3.9–7.2 kHz). More 'digital static' than the other loading SFX. 
Used to indicate when something is counting or loading



Ui long zoom. 
0.86s hi-hat-like sweep, very bright (centroid 6.1 kHz), noisy (ZCR 0.74). Sounds like a fast digital reverse/zoom. 
camera push-ins, Digital push-in / dolly-zoom 

Wooden floor board creak. 


1.08s creak — slow attack (0.82s) as the creak rises, then a quick drop. Broad band 190 Hz – 6.8 kHz. 
Tension-build before a punchline. 'You're not gonna believe this…' beat. Pairs with a slow zoom-in on the face. 
Wooden floor board (faster)
0.59s shorter, faster wooden creak (centroid 2 kHz, 0.48s attack). Roughly half the length of the standard floor-board creak. 
Always use for a camera push in. When there is a camera zoom in to the subject in the video/


wooden floor creaking. 


0.96s creak — similar character to 'Wooden floor board creak' but with a faster onset (0.45s vs 0.82s). 
Tension-build before a punchline. 'You're not gonna believe this…' beat. Pairs with a slow zoom-in on the face. Same role as the other floor creak pick whichever timing matches your edit. Use this one if your tension beat is brief. 

Awww. 


1.3s crowd 'aaawww' vocal. Mid-range (centroid ~1.2 kHz), warm decay (~0.46s). Sympathetic / 'oh no' tone, not harsh. 
Sympathetic reaction beat. "That's sad" / "poor guy" / “so cute” moment. 

ceeday huh. 


0.35s vocal 'huh?' — the Ceeday meme sample. Mid frequency (~1.3 kHz), short 
When the subject says something that is confusing. Used when that part has a Confused / 'what??' reaction over a meme jump-cut. 

counter. 


0.59s very bright high-frequency tick (centroid 6.8 kHz). Highly tonal (autocorr 0.84) — feels like a small digital counter tick. 
When talking about something in the script that contains a number flip / count-up overlay. E.g., “the points adds up”, when talking about point adding up or gathering rewards, use the counter

Iphone notification. 


0.56s, the iconic iPhone tri-tone (3 ascending notes). Extremely tonal (autocorr 0.96). Mid-band 290 Hz – 5.8 kHz. 
Text / DM / push-notification graphic landing on screen. Use it ONCE per appearance — don't stack. e.g. on-screen iMessage graphic, text-from-mom skit, or a fake DM screenshot 

pop-1. 


0.15s round bubble pop. Mid frequency (~1.2 kHz), no noise. 
When a B-roll pops on the screen, a button highlights, sticker pop, emoji appearing, and dot point bullet landing. 

Ting. 


0.70s clean single-note ting (centroid 2.8 kHz, very tonal). 
Text / DM / push-notification graphic landing on screen. Use it ONCE per appearance—don't stack. Also use for bullet-point reveal.

Ting !!! 2.  


0.66s bright bell ting with a long metallic ring-out. Percussive attack, sustained decay. 
Used when the subject or the person in the video says something awkward.

chair squeaking. 


1.25s high-pitched squeak (centroid 3.9 kHz, noisy). Slow rising attack (~1s). 
Used for an awkward situation

Keyboard press. 


0.14s mechanical key clack. Bright (4.4 kHz), noisy, instant. 
Typing beats — letter-by-letter caption reveal, typewriter-style text drop. Stack 3–4 of these in sequence for a 'typing' moment. 
Keyboard typing 
6.5s loop of multiple key clacks layering on top of each other. Mid-band (520 Hz – 6.9 kHz), noisy. A full 'someone typing a paragraph' bed. 
Use when talking about something to searched for. This is also used if the speaker says to search for something, the on screen text will be what will be searched for, with a typing in animation and this keyboard typing sound effect


Phone typing
11.1s very long soft thumbtyping on a phone screen. Mid-band, very low ZCR (0.04) — feels muffled and rhythmic, not clacky. 
Used when talking about something that has to be typed. For example, if the speaker says to search for something, the on screen text will be what will be searched for, with a typing in animation and this phone typing sound effect.

rizz sound. 


1.04s low pitched 'rizzz' meme tone (centroid 457 Hz). Tonal (autocorr 0.74), low-band 44 Hz – 1.7 kHz. 
Confident punch-in / glow-up moment. Drop on a meme cut where the speaker is being 'too smooth'.
